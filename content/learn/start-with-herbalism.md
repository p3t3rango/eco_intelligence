There's a romantic image of herbalism: a basket, a sunny meadow, a handful of something wild brewed into tea by nightfall. The reality is quieter, and better. Herbalism starts as a practice of **attention** — noticing what grows around you, season to season, long before anything ends up in a cup.

So if you're just beginning, here's the most useful advice we can give: **slow down, and read first.**

## Get to know a few plants deeply

You don't need to memorize a field guide. Pick **three plants** that already grow near you — maybe yarrow, plantain, or a culinary herb on your windowsill — and follow them for a season. Watch how they change. Learn their look-alikes. This kind of slow familiarity is worth more than a hundred half-remembered names.

> The goal isn't to harvest everything. It's to belong to a place enough to know what's there.

## Lean on trustworthy sources

The internet is full of confident, unsourced claims about what herbs "cure." Be skeptical, and anchor yourself to evidence-based references:

- The NIH's [**Herbs at a Glance**](https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/herbsataglance) gives plain-language, research-backed profiles — including what *isn't* well supported.
- Memorial Sloan Kettering's [**About Herbs**](https://www.mskcc.org/cancer-care/diagnosis-treatment/symptom-management/integrative-medicine/herbs) database, written by pharmacists, covers interactions and safety.

A good beginner's book helps too — Rosemary Gladstar's *Medicinal Herbs: A Beginner's Guide* is a gentle, widely loved place to start.

## A note on safety

Plants are chemistry. "Natural" does not mean "harmless," and some plants interact with medications or are unsafe in pregnancy. This is educational writing, not medical advice — talk to a qualified practitioner before using any herb medicinally, and never harvest a plant you can't identify with confidence.

The best classroom is the patch of earth you already have. [Analyze your yard](/grow) to see what's growing there, and let your herbalism begin where you live.
