If you do one thing for the land you tend, make it this: **plant natives.**

A native plant evolved alongside the insects, birds, fungi, and soil microbes of your specific region. Those relationships are precise. A monarch caterpillar can only eat milkweed. Many native bees can only use the pollen of plants they co-evolved with. Swap in an ornamental from another continent and it may look lovely — but to the local food web, it can be a green desert.

> Your yard is small. The number of yards is not. Restoration adds up.

## Start by learning what belongs

"Native" is regional and specific, so ground yourself in good references:

- The [**USDA PLANTS Database**](https://plants.usda.gov/) gives authoritative native status and range maps.
- The [**Lady Bird Johnson Wildflower Center**](https://www.wildflower.org/) has a searchable native-plant database and how-to guides by region.
- [**United Plant Savers**](https://unitedplantsavers.org/) tracks which native *medicinal* plants are at risk — a beautiful place to aim your efforts.

## Help something that's struggling

The most meaningful gardens do a job. Find out what's declining near you — a pollinator, a bird, an at-risk plant — and plant for *it*. A patch of native milkweed becomes a monarch waystation. A succession of native blooms feeds bees from spring to frost. Small, specific, real.

This is exactly what your plan is built around: when you [analyze your yard](/grow), we ground every suggestion in the species actually observed near you, and flag where your patch can help a local problem.

Plant what belongs. The rest of the web will come back to meet it.
