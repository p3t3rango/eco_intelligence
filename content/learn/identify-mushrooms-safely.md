Fungi are the quiet engine of a healthy yard — breaking down wood, feeding soil, partnering with roots underground. Learning to see them changes how you look at the ground entirely.

It's also the one area where we'll be blunt: **some mushrooms can kill you, and their deadly look-alikes can fool beginners and experts alike.** So we start mycology the safe way — as observers first.

## Identify for the joy of it, not the plate

For your first season (or three), treat every find as a puzzle to *identify*, not to eat. Photograph it from multiple angles — cap, gills, stem, base — note where it's growing and on what. You'll be amazed how much you learn with nothing at stake.

> Foragers have a saying: there are old mushroom hunters, and bold mushroom hunters, but no old, bold mushroom hunters.

## Learn with a community

The single safest move in mycology is **not learning alone**:

- The [**North American Mycological Association**](https://www.namyco.org/) lists local clubs and forays — real people who'll teach you in person.
- [**Mushroom Observer**](https://mushroomobserver.org/) is a community archive where you can compare your finds against thousands of identified specimens.
- [**iNaturalist**](https://www.inaturalist.org/taxa/47170-Fungi) will show you what fungi are being seen near you and help crowd-source an ID.

## The rule that keeps you alive

**Never eat any wild mushroom unless an experienced, in-person identifier has confirmed it.** Photos and apps are for learning, not for clearing something to eat. When in doubt, admire it and leave it.
